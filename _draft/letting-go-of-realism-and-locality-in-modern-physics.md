Abstract

This paper reinterprets the major theories of modern physics (quantum mechanics, relativity, classical mechanics) from the perspective of non-dualism found in Upanishadic philosophy and Buddhism. Elements of physical models, often conceived as static and independent "entities," are redefined as interdependently generated and changing processes, depicted as a rule set shared by the whole. Within this framework, quantum phenomena such as wave function collapse, the measurement problem, and quantum entanglement are re-examined as process updates or reconstructions of relationships. Quantum non-local correlations suggested by Bell's inequality are also considered natural phenomena from a non-dualistic viewpoint by relaxing assumptions of realism and locality. Furthermore, based on this worldview of interdependent processes, unresolved problems such as the unification of quantum gravity theory, the resolution of the measurement problem, the interpretation of dark matter/dark energy, the origin of the arrow of time (irreversibility), and the black hole information paradox are repositioned. Additionally, it is confirmed that this interpretative shift is consistent with fundamental theorems like the no-signalling principle, microcausality, and Noether's theorem, demonstrating that the proposed model does not contradict current physical theories. This process-based model, while maintaining interpretative novelty and mathematical coherence, has the potential to question the philosophical presuppositions of existing theories and open paths to new research programs.

Introduction

Since classical physics, natural science has described the world as a collection of substantial elements. For example, in Newtonian mechanics, particles and objects are considered entities existing independently of others, and mechanical phenomena arise from their interactions. However, physics in the 20th century and beyond has revealed properties that cannot be grasped by the view of independent entities, such as the interaction between spacetime and matter in relativity, and entanglement (non-local correlations between distant particles) in quantum mechanics [ref] [ref]. These findings suggest a new worldview based on "relationships" and "processes."

Interestingly, such an interdependent worldview has long been emphasized in the traditions of Eastern philosophy. Upanishadic philosophy (8th-5th centuries BCE) teaches that Brahman (the ultimate reality of the universe) and Atman (the essence of the individual) are fundamentally one (Brahman-Atman identity), and that the diverse existences in the phenomenal world are merely manifestations of ultimate reality. This non-dualistic insight resonates with the holistic perspectives shown by modern cosmology and quantum physics [ref]. Furthermore, Buddhist philosophy, particularly the concept of Pratītyasamutpāda (dependent origination) in Mahayana Buddhism, posits that all phenomena arise in dependence on other factors, and no independent, immutable entity exists [ref]. This idea of dependent origination has been pointed out to be common with complex systems and ecosystem theories in modern science, as well as quantum entanglement phenomena [ref] [ref].

This paper is an attempt to reinterpret the theoretical structure of modern physics as interdependent processes, based on this Eastern non-dualistic worldview. Specifically, the fundamental elements in quantum mechanics, relativity, and classical mechanics are redefined not as static "material existences" but as processes constantly updated within relationships, and are re-conceived as a set of rules (laws) shared by a universal interaction network. Based on this redefinition, difficult problems such as wave function collapse due to quantum measurement, the measurement problem, and entanglement between particles are re-examined from the perspective of "state update in the temporal development of processes" and "reconstruction of relationships." Furthermore, regarding the non-local correlations between particles suggested by the violation of Bell's inequality, the conventional realistic and local viewpoint is abandoned, and phenomena are interpreted as occurring in "one system as a whole," following Upanishadic monism and the Buddhist idea of Śūnyatā (emptiness). Moreover, this process-based worldview aims to provide new insights into several unresolved problems facing physics. Issues such as the unification of quantum mechanics and gravitational theory (quantum gravity), the measurement problem and the objective collapse hypothesis of the wave function, the nature of dark matter/dark energy, the origin of the arrow of time related to the second law of thermodynamics, and the black hole information paradox will be reconsidered from the perspective of interdependent processes. The structure of this paper is as follows: first, an overview of the philosophical and physical background; next, the framework of process-based redefinition and the interpretation of phenomena based on it; then, a discussion of the implications for the unresolved problems mentioned above; and finally, a discussion of the theoretical consistency and future prospects of this proposal.

Theoretical Background

Non-dualistic Perspectives in Eastern Thought

Upanishadic philosophy is the wellspring of Indian philosophy that discusses the oneness of ultimate reality and the apparent diversity of the phenomenal world. In the Upanishads, the absolute reality pervading the foundation of all phenomena is called Brahman, and it is taught that the inner self of humans (Atman) and Brahman are essentially identical. According to this idea of Brahman-Atman identity, individual things and events perceived daily are all ultimately expressions of Brahman and are not entities that exist independently on their own. The phenomenal world is also termed **Māyā (illusion)**, and its substantiality is considered merely relative and illusory. Therefore, in Upanishadic non-dualism, all existence is united at a fundamental level, and dualistic distinctions such as subject and object, matter and consciousness are ultimately transcended.

In Buddhism as well, particularly in the Madhyamaka school of Mahayana Buddhism, the philosophy of non-duality and selflessness (anātman/niḥsvabhāva) was deepened. Madhyamaka thought, represented by Nāgārjuna, posits that all things lack a fixed, unchanging nature (svabhāva) and that everything arises provisionally within a dependent relationship with countless other factors, i.e., dependent origination [ref]. This is known as the philosophy of **Śūnyatā (emptiness)**, meaning the truth that all existences are empty, i.e., not independent entities. For example, a single lotus flower arises only when countless conditions (hetu-pratyaya) such as water, soil, sunlight, seeds, and climate are met, and it is not an entity that exists independently on its own. From this standpoint of dependent origination and emptiness, the world is understood not as a collection of fixed things but as a series of mutually dependent processes. The insight in the *Heart Sutra* that the five skandhas (the five aggregates composing a human being) are all empty also suggests that all existence, including the self, is a fluid, relational process.

It is noteworthy that these non-dualistic perspectives of Eastern philosophy resonate suggestively with cutting-edge areas of modern physics such as quantum theory and cosmology [ref]. For example, Buddhist dependent origination, which states that "all phenomena arise in dependence on other phenomena," has common ground with the phenomenon of quantum entanglement, where spatially separated particles mutually depend on each other's states [ref]. Indeed, it has been pointed out that the concept of entanglement in quantum mechanics—"once two particles have interacted, no matter how far apart they subsequently become, the state of one can instantaneously affect the state of the other"—is consistent with the Buddhist principle of dependent origination that "all phenomena arise in dependence on others" [ref]. Similarly, the quantum worldview, which, as Bell's theorem shows, forces us to abandon the assumption of local realism, is also compatible with the Upanishadic monistic view that "ultimately there is only one reality." This paper aims to take such non-dualism of Eastern thought not merely as a metaphor but as a kind of ontological hint, and apply it to the reinterpretation of modern physical theories.

Ontological Assumptions in Modern Physics Models

Before delving into a non-dualistic reinterpretation, it is necessary to organize the ontological frameworks presupposed by each of the major theories of modern physics. Classical mechanics, relativity, and quantum mechanics each deal with different scales and phenomena, but in traditional interpretations, they all tend to describe the world as the behavior of some kind of "entity."

In **classical mechanics (Newtonian mechanics)**, objects such as mass points and rigid bodies are considered to move through spacetime with definite positions and momenta, and forces act as interactions between these objects. Here, objects are entities that exist in themselves, and interactions (forces) are considered secondary relationships attendant to them [ref]. In the Newtonian worldview, the universe was seen as a collection of independent "things," an order mechanically linked. In this view, relationships are merely incidental (e.g., the force of gravity), and the reality of things themselves is fundamental. The idea that "each object is essentially independent and self-existent, and its relationship with others is secondary" has long been dominant as a basic intuition of Western science [ref].

In the theory of relativity (especially general relativity), space and time are integrated into an inseparable continuum called spacetime, and furthermore, gravity is understood as the geometry (curvature) of spacetime. In general relativity, there is a bidirectional dynamic relationship where the presence of matter (energy density) curves spacetime, and this curved spacetime governs the motion of matter. J.A. Wheeler succinctly expressed this mutual relationship as "Spacetime tells matter how to move; matter tells spacetime how to curve" [ref]. From the perspective of relativity, the gravitational field and matter are inseparable and mutually dependent, exhibiting a characteristic as if relationship itself has become substantial, which was absent in classical mechanics. Nevertheless, even in relativistic cosmology, celestial bodies such as stars and galaxies exist as individual entities (their collective distribution curves spacetime), and spacetime itself tends to be regarded as the reality of a "field," a continuum. That is, while relativity describes the interaction between "matter" and "spacetime," it implicitly assumes both as independent ontological categories (field and matter).

Quantum mechanics is the theory that most overturned conventional views of reality in 20th-century physics. In quantum mechanics, particles have wave-like properties, and physical quantities are not determined until observed, existing in a probabilistic superposition state (in the case of the Copenhagen interpretation). The wave function $\Psi$ describes the state of the system, but there are various debates about whether it is a physical reality or merely a representation of knowledge. In the standard interpretation, observation (measurement) causes the wave function to collapse to a specific eigenstate, but the substantial meaning of this process (what is "actually" happening) is unclear, leading to the difficult problem known as the measurement problem. In addition, it has been confirmed through experiments by Aspect et al. [ref] that strong correlations (entanglement) can exist between spatially separated particles, and that explaining this requires abandoning local realism. In other words, a consequence of quantum theory is the suggestion that **"locally determined reality" cannot exist in principle. This point contradicted Einstein's view of causality and was received with great surprise (as ridiculed by the phrase "spooky action at a distance"). However, strangely enough, the non-local correlations of quantum mechanics do not enable superluminal communication of information and remain merely statistical correlations, thus not contradicting the causality of special relativity (the speed of light limit) [ref]. The quantum world exhibits a non-local correlation structure, but observable information transmission maintains locality**, showing a seemingly contradictory characteristic.

As described above, the various theories of modern physics have successfully depicted the world at different levels, but the underlying ontological assumptions (independent entities vs. relationships) are not unified. For this reason, it can be said that conceptual frictions such as "quantum gravity where the spacetime background (coordinate system) cannot be fixed" and the measurement problem/information paradox arise in the integration of quantum mechanics and relativity. This paper aims to shed new light on these problems by reconstructing this ontological premise itself from a non-dualistic standpoint.

From Static Entities to Interdependent Processes: Framework for Model Redefinition

This chapter details an approach to redefine the frameworks of quantum mechanics, relativity, and classical mechanics as interdependent processes. The key is to regard what has conventionally been considered an "entity" as an event that appears only within relationships with others at a more fundamental level [ref] [ref]. In this view, the basic unit of physical phenomena is not a fixed existence but the process of interaction between systems itself.

Reinterpreting Quantum Mechanics: From States to Relations

The core of the process-based reinterpretation in quantum mechanics lies in not regarding "quantum states" or "wave functions" as objective realities themselves, but as mere tools for describing relationships [ref]. That is, a quantum state encodes the relationship between an observer (or measuring device) and the observed object, and is a relative attribute that actualizes only at the moment of interaction [ref]. This is also called the relational interpretation and is consistent with the view of Relational Quantum Mechanics (RQM) proposed by Carlo Rovelli and others [ref] [ref]. In the relational interpretation, quantum states are not objective realities but auxiliary concepts, and the basis of physical reality is the physical systems (systems) entering into interaction and the values of physical quantities they can take [ref]. The important point is that the values of physical quantities (observation results) are determined only at the discrete moments of interaction [ref]. During periods without interaction (while the system is isolated), it is considered that the physical quantity has no value (is undetermined). In this view, events occurring in the universe are a collection of a series of sparse events (discrete interaction events), and in each event, a physical quantity takes a specific value between one system and another [ref]. And that value is relative to the interacting partner (system) [ref]. For example, the attribute of an electron's spin is determined as "up" or "down" relatively within the process of interaction with a measuring device, and is not an attribute that exists independently prior to interaction.

Adopting such a relationship-重視 (relationship-重視) view naturally alleviates the measurement problem in quantum mechanics. In the conventional Copenhagen interpretation, it was difficult to explain why a probabilistic wave function collapses to a single value upon observation. However, from a relational perspective, "collapse" is not an objective physical collapse but merely an update of the description of another system's state from the perspective of one system [ref]. For the observer, the state of the other system becomes uniquely determined by measurement (information is updated), but from the perspective of a third system, a superposition state still holds—such relative situations are permissible [ref]. In fact, RQM allows any physical system to play the role of an "observer," enabling all interactions to be described quantum mechanically without assuming special "consciousness" or "classical measuring devices" [ref]. This allows a consistent explanation of the phenomena brought about by observation without introducing an objective collapse mechanism for the wave function (such as hidden variables or many-worlds) [ref]. In short, by weakening the reality of quantum states and assigning a role to relationships, the measurement problem is reduced to a "difference in perspective" and ceases to be a paradox [ref] [ref].

In this stance, where wave function collapse is regarded as "process update," the time evolution of a quantum system is also understood as the propagation of information (relationships). The unitary time evolution by the Schrödinger equation merely describes the change in relationships in an isolated system and can be considered a process essentially equivalent to state update by interaction (observation). From an extreme viewpoint, the "wave function" is like informational glue connecting each interaction event and is not a persistent entity in itself. Under this interpretation, quantum entanglement can also be thought of not as two spatially separated, independently existing particles mysteriously uniting, but rather as a process generated initially as a single interacting system later projecting and appearing at two locations. In other words, a pair of entangled particles are two aspects of a single system, and their relationship is merely actualized all at once by observation, so the distance between particles is not essential. This allows the non-local correlation of entanglement to be seen not as "causeless action between two independent objects" but as "internal correlation within a single system." As discussed later, this non-locality, known through Bell's theorem, can also be interpreted as a suggestion from quantum theory that the whole is more real than a mere sum of its parts, and indeed, some philosophers and physicists point out that "the deepest discovery of quantum theory is that isolated reality does not exist, and relationship itself is reality" [ref].

Reinterpreting Relativity: Spacetime as a Dynamic Relational Process

General relativity originally has a dynamic view of spacetime, but under a process worldview, it goes a step further to regard spacetime and gravity themselves as phenomena arising from relationships with others. That is, it ceases to absolutize spacetime as a background and views the gravitational field (geometric spacetime structure) as a relative process established through interaction with matter.

The equivalence principle and field equations (Einstein's equations) of general relativity have a duality: "the curvature of spacetime (gravity) is determined by matter-energy, and the motion of matter follows the curvature of spacetime" [ref]. As Wheeler's words [ref] suggest, both are interdependent, and at this point, the classical entity view has already been considerably modified. However, in a process interpretation, the possibility that spacetime itself emerges from something else is further considered. In fact, in recent theoretical physics, the view that spacetime emerges from a more fundamental quantum informational structure is becoming increasingly influential [ref] [ref]. A famous example is research based on the holographic principle (AdS/CFT correspondence), where the information of spacetime is encoded in a lower-dimensional quantum theory (CFT on the boundary), and the entanglement (quantum entanglement structure) of the quantum system is thought to construct the geometry of spacetime [ref] [ref]. For example, in a model where 5-dimensional spacetime is constructed from 4-dimensional quantum theory, it is expressed that "the entire spatial region is spun out by the interactions (entanglement) between the components of the quantum system" [ref]. In such a scheme, spacetime is not a static container but can be regarded as a self-organized pattern of quantum processes (interaction network).

In short, by thoroughly applying process thinking to the relativistic cosmological view, the distinction between spacetime and matter also becomes secondary, and an image emerges where both arise simultaneously from interaction itself at a fundamental level. The gravitational field is not created by matter but is the inseparable relationship with matter itself, and spacetime does not exist independently of matter. To put it extremely, "both matter and spacetime are merely patterns woven by a deeper level of processes that encompass them." This view is also consistent with trends in quantum gravity theory. For example, in loop quantum gravity, space is represented by a discrete network (spin network), and geometry is determined by their relationships. Furthermore, approaches that consider space itself as a process, such as Wheeler's "quantum foam" model allowing for topological changes in spacetime, have been explored [ref]. The process worldview philosophically underpins these theoretical attempts and provides a unified picture that "space, time, and matter are essentially relational structures woven by events."

Reinterpreting Classical Mechanics: Independent Entities as Mere Approximations

How does the process interpretation explain the fact that in the classical world (macroscopic scale), objects behave as if they were independent entities? The key is scale and approximation. When the network of interdependent processes becomes very large in scale, locally stable patterns and repetitive structures emerge. These stable structures are what we recognize as "objects" or "particles," and they are not fixed entities themselves but rather nodal points of stable processes. For example, a single electron can exist as a fairly long-lived stable structure if there is no interaction with its surroundings, so it can be regarded as a kind of entity. However, an electron can also fluctuate due to interaction with the electromagnetic field (e.g., radiation reaction), and at high energies, it can transform into complex processes by creating and annihilating other particles. Elementary particles are not "hard, unchanging spheres" but open existences that allow interaction with the environment, and in that sense, can be regarded as endpoints of processes.

The same applies to macroscopic objects. For example, the human body constantly undergoes processes of material metabolism and energy exchange with the environment and does not exist completely independently. Thermodynamically, it is an open system, and life activities cannot be maintained without entropy exchange with the surroundings. Moreover, not limited to life, all macro objects are, quantum mechanically, aggregates of an enormous number of degrees of freedom (e.g., a collection of as many as $10^{23}$ particles), and within them, fluctuations and interactions are constantly occurring. However, from the outside, these are averaged out and decohered, appearing as a stable single object. In the process of decoherence, information about microscopic relationships, such as quantum entanglement, diffuses into the environment, and the system comes to exhibit classical behavior [ref] [ref]. As a result, objects take on a definite state as if they were independent of others (acquiring objective reality). However, behind this lies constant information exchange (process) with the environment, and in reality, they are not isolated from their surroundings.

Based on the above, classical mechanical reality is positioned as a special limit of process-based reality. That is, the classical world is the limit where numerous interaction processes are averaged and appear as a smooth continuum. In this view, the classical concept of an "independent entity" is by no means strict, but a pseudo-entity composed of a network of relationships when decomposed. Therefore, ideal concepts assumed in classical mechanics, such as inertial frames and rigid bodies, are ultimately only approximations of the global patterns (ordered behavior) of interdependent processes. For example, a rigid body appears to move as a single unit due to the strong bonds between its internal particles, but this is the result of internal processes constantly maintaining harmony. If the energy of internal processes (e.g., lattice vibrations) increases, the rigid body will disintegrate and become fluid like a liquid or gas. In other words, if relationships change, the boundaries of entities also change, and independent entities are not fixed categories. In a process model, even the identity of physical objects is considered not fixed but capable of transforming with time evolution. Such a perspective resonates with the "process philosophy" approach recently gaining attention in biology and complex systems science and forces a profound shift in how we perceive the material world.

Reconsidering Wave Function Collapse and Entanglement: Observation as a Process

Based on the framework above, we provide a reinterpretation of several phenomena unique to quantum mechanics. This section attempts to re-read wave function collapse, the measurement problem, and non-local correlation phenomena due to entanglement from a process perspective.

The Process of Observation (Measurement) and Wave Function Collapse

Quantum measurement is a process at the core of the interpretation problem in quantum mechanics. Traditionally, it is known that measurement causes a quantum state to probabilistically converge (collapse), but this has been treated as a discontinuous assumption, separate from the unitary time evolution according to the Schrödinger equation, and difficult to confirm experimentally. In the process interpretation, measurement is considered an essentially continuous phenomenon with other interactions [ref]. That is, the entire series of processes where interaction occurs between the measuring device and the quantum system, they become entangled, and then a specific indication appears on the device, is considered the "observation" event, and within that, the state of the quantum system is determined relative to the device (appears to collapse) [ref]. At this time, the information obtained by a specific system (observer) is confirmed, but the possibility remains that a probabilistic description still holds for other systems. In fact, Schrödinger's cat and Wigner's friend paradoxes show that the description of the result can differ depending on who is considered the observer, but in the relational interpretation, this is simply understood as relativity between viewpoints [ref]. It can be shown that for any observer, no violated causality or logical contradiction arises [ref]. In other words, measurement is always an update of relationships, and an objective "collapse event" does not occur simultaneously throughout spacetime. From this perspective, the appearance of observation results can be consistently explained without introducing special dynamics to describe quantum measurement (e.g., objective collapse processes like GRW theory).

The role of the wave function in observation also changes. The wave function can be interpreted as a set of potential information that one system has about another. If an interaction occurs with an observation partner (measuring device), one result is realized from that potential information and shared as new information by both interacting parties. This realization of information is the essence of wave function collapse, and it is not an image of the system itself objectively jumping. Rather, observation is grasped as "an instantaneous process where relationships are actualized all at once." The fact that the result becomes unique immediately after observation can be explained by this view of relationship update, without assuming multiverses or the like.

Relational Interpretation of Entanglement and Non-local Correlations

Quantum entanglement is a symbolic phenomenon when considering a process worldview. In a state where two or more particles are quantumly entangled, strong correlations exist between the particles even if they are spatially distant, and the measurement result of one instantaneously determines the state of the other [ref]. Bell's theorem showed that to explain this correlation, either the particles do not have local reality (pre-determined values for results) until the moment of observation, or information is exchanged between particles faster than the speed of light. Experimental verification has shown that the latter (superluminal signal transmission) does not occur [ref], so ultimately the conclusion that **"objective local reality does not exist (at least within the scope of quantum mechanics)"** is supported. This is a very radical conclusion, and "hidden variable" models based on local realism, which Einstein and others aimed for, have been excluded.

However, from a process worldview perspective, the experimental results of non-locality are rather intuitively understandable. This is because if we abandon the view of reality that regards the world as a "collection of separate entities" and see the entire world as a single interaction network that permeates it, then even with spatial separation, it can be explained as a correlation within the overall system. In Buddhist dependent origination and Huayan thought, there is the metaphor of Indra's net, expressed as "one is all, all is one." The universe is like a network of countless jewels (events) mutually reflecting each other, where the whole is reflected in one, and one is reflected in the whole. This metaphor is surprisingly similar to the situation of quantum entanglement. That is, when two particles A and B are entangled, information about B is latent in the state of A, and information about A is latent in the state of B. When A is determined to be in state $a_i$ by observation, B is simultaneously determined to be in state $b_i$, and this can be understood as A and B not being independent entities but two aspects of a unified system. The violation of Bell's inequality can also be read as experimental evidence supporting such holistic reality. As some point out, "According to quantum theory, relationship itself is more real than individuals" [ref], non-locality suggests that the reality of the world is network-like [ref].

Borrowing the words of Advaita Vedanta (Indian philosophy), "In Brahman (the sole reality of the universe), all is one." The non-local correlation of quantum entanglement may indeed be a manifestation of Brahman's unity appearing through the veil of Māyā (illusion) of spatial separation. From the Buddhist standpoint, since all dharmas (phenomena) are established by dependent origination (= exist only in dependence on others), there is nothing strange about distant particles existing in a state of mutual dependence [ref]. Rather, independence itself is an illusion, and instantaneous correlation due to entanglement can be considered "possible precisely because there is no substance." Thus, non-local quantum correlation phenomena become extremely natural consequences in non-dualistic philosophy. Entanglement, which appears as an eerie action contrary to causality in classical intuition, is merely self-correlation from the perspective that the whole is one.

What is even more interesting is that the non-local correlation of quantum theory remains informationally hidden. That is, an entangled particle pair has strong correlations, but it cannot be used to transmit messages by itself [ref]. This can also be understood by analogy with the "Two Truths Doctrine" (ultimate truth and conventional truth) in Buddhism. At the level of ultimate truth (paramārtha-satya), everything is one, but at the level of the everyday world (saṃvṛti-satya), causality and relative distinctions hold. Similarly, in the quantum world, even if overall consistency is maintained, this consistency does not directly appear at the level of classical reality that we can observe, and a local causal structure is maintained. As a result, the non-locality of quantum mechanics is a potential (as a hidden variable) non-locality and has a two-layered structure that does not affect manifest causal connections [ref]. The non-dualistic interpretation can regard this duality as two sides of a single whole, helping to intuitively understand the compatibility of quantum-level wholeness and classical-level locality.

In summary, wave function collapse and entanglement can both be uniformly understood as **"events where relationships change form"** in the worldview of interdependent processes. Both state determination by observation and non-local correlation by entanglement are fundamentally not about "independent entities changing" but "the overall network structure being reorganized." This understanding simplifies points that were incomprehensible in conventional interpretations and allows us to accept the strangeness of quantum mechanics as philosophically natural.

Repositioning Unsolved Problems from the Perspective of Interdependent Processes

This section approaches unsolved problems in modern physics from the perspective of interdependent processes. Problems that were difficult with conventional approaches can be re-examined from a different angle under the new ontology and worldview constructed in the previous chapter. Here, we will specifically discuss the following issues: unification of quantum gravity, the measurement problem and collapse hypothesis, interpretation of dark matter/dark energy, the origin of the arrow of time (irreversibility), and the black hole information paradox. For each, we will state suggestions and directions for resolution based on a process worldview.

Unifying Quantum Gravity: Bridging through a Relational Approach

The unification of quantum mechanics and general relativity (construction of quantum gravity theory) is one of the greatest unsolved problems in physics. The contradiction between the two theories is often said to lie in the fact that "quantum theory is formulated on a fixed spacetime background, whereas in relativity, spacetime itself is dynamic." The process worldview can be said to be a position that attempts to bridge the two by removing this dependence on the background. On the quantum theory side, as already mentioned, relationships, not objective entities, are fundamental (a fixed spatial background is also unnecessary). On the relativity side as well, spacetime is regarded as a dynamic structure arising from its relationship with matter (not a fixed background). Combining these two perspectives suggests a deeper process structure that encompasses both spacetime and quantum phenomena, which could become the unified basis for both theories.

Actual approaches include, for example, holography and emergent spacetime theories. In the holographic principle, "a theory of spacetime including gravity" corresponds to "a quantum theory without gravity" (CFT on the boundary), and in the latter, even without spacetime, the quantum entanglement structure reproduces the spacetime of the former [ref] [ref]. This is precisely a scheme where relationships (quantum entanglement) give rise to geometry (spacetime), consistent with the process worldview. Furthermore, in Erik Verlinde's proposed emergent gravity, spacetime and the gravitational field are said to arise from the entropic properties of information in a vacuum [ref]. According to his theory, spacetime and gravity emerge together from the entanglement structure of underlying micro-degrees of freedom [ref], and phenomena like dark energy and dark matter can also be explained as effective changes in the laws of gravity [ref]. This is also a view of gravity as a surface effect of relationships and can be called a non-dualistic idea. The process worldview brings together these various approaches to quantum gravity under one philosophical umbrella and provides a guideline for a unified theory that does not assume entities.

In summary, the key to unifying quantum gravity is to eliminate the concept of independent entities (point-like particles or spacetime points) from the description of the world as much as possible and shift to a network-like process description. If even the concept of spatial distance is regarded as a byproduct of relationships, problems such as singularities and spacetime discretization at the Planck scale could also be interpreted from different angles. For example, the Big Bang singularity could also be regarded as a kind of phase-transition-like network reorganization process, leading to the construction of theories that dynamically treat the very generation of time and space. The process worldview can assist in bridging quantum and cosmic phenomena in a way that appeals to human intuition and can provide inspiration for new theoretical developments.

The Measurement Problem and Collapse Hypotheses: Resolution through Relativizing Observation

The measurement problem in quantum mechanics is the difficult question of how to treat the act of observation within physical theory. In standard methods, measurement is posited as an operation that causes the system to jump non-unitarily, requiring classical treatment of measuring devices, etc. Objective collapse hypotheses (such as GRW theory) are approaches that introduce probabilistic wave function collapse as a physical process, but they require additional parameters or hidden mechanisms and have not yet reached definitive verification.

In the process interpretation, the act of measurement is seen as continuous with other interactions, so there is no need to treat measurement specially [ref]. By describing an extended system including the observer with quantum theory and positioning the observation result as an update of relationships (realization of information), the measurement problem shifts from "where does collapse occur?" to "how are relationships determined?". In this frame, collapse does not occur objectively, and only the description of the state changes for each observer, so agreement between multiple observers (e.g., Wigner's friend paradox) can also be explained without contradiction if considered relatively [ref]. In short, the measurement problem is likely to be resolved if the relativity of relationships is acknowledged.

From this viewpoint, the necessity of reifying collapse hypotheses also diminishes. For example, the many-worlds interpretation of quantum mechanics branches the universe to avoid collapse, but in the process interpretation, a relative many-worlds view where "the state is different for each observer" is sufficient, and there is no necessity to physically generate countless universes. Similarly, idealistic claims such as consciousness collapsing the wave function can be incorporated as a type of relationship (mental process), eliminating the need to assume special principles of action. In other words, the act of observation can be endogenously incorporated into the universe, which could be a major paradigm shift in the foundations of quantum mechanics.

Dark Matter/Dark Energy: Unknowns Arising from Overlooked Processes

Dark matter and dark energy, inferred from calculations of the total energy of the universe, are postulated as undiscovered entities (unknown particles or fields). However, from a process perspective, these might be signs that our models are overlooking some interaction processes. That is, there is a possibility that we are treating matter and energy too substantially and are treating the effects of underlying relationships as compensatory terms.

For example, dark energy acts like a repulsive force causing the accelerated expansion of the universe, which also matches the effect of the energy of the quantum vacuum (zero-point energy). The quantum vacuum is a dynamic field of interaction where particle-antiparticle pairs are constantly being created and annihilated; it is not mere "nothingness." In the process interpretation, the vacuum is also part of the structure woven by rich processes, and its macroscopic behavior (cosmological constant-like action) may be appearing as an energy term. Dark energy might be re-envisioned not as unknown energy but as a global manifestation of known processes (vacuum fluctuations). Similarly, for dark matter, there are theories that seek the cause of gravity acting stronger than expected on galactic scales not in unknown particles but in information structures or entropic effects at spatial scales [ref]. In Verlinde's theory, the reason the laws of gravity change with scale is attributed to a scale-dependent transition in the structure of micro-entanglement [ref] [ref]. According to this explanation, dark matter does not need to exist for observations to be consistent. In other words, the dark matter phenomenon is merely the result of the effective laws of gravity changing due to the hierarchical structure of relationships [ref].

These are just examples, but applying a process worldview leads to the idea of exploring "unseen relationships/processes" rather than successively postulating "invisible entities." Perhaps the currently termed dark energy/mass problems are manifestations of our inability to correctly incorporate the contributions of the entire universe's interaction network (e.g., the quantum entanglement web). If the effects of universal interconnectedness influence local motion, they should have been overlooked by conventional independent system approximations. Process models, by viewing the entire universe as a single network, can easily incorporate such global effects into theory. As a new perspective on dark matter/energy problems, an approach that discards entity assumptions and reasons inductively from the overall process may progress in the future.

The Arrow of Time and Irreversibility: The Temporal Structure of Relational Networks

The reason why time has a direction (i.e., a unidirectional flow from past to future is selected) is one of the fundamental problems in physics. Although the law of entropy increase by the second law of thermodynamics provides an empirical arrow, the basic equations are time-reversal symmetric, and why the past and future are asymmetric has been a difficult problem to understand.

In recent years, the possibility that quantum entanglement holds the key to this arrow of time has been discussed [ref] [ref]. According to research, the monotonic increase of entanglement (quantum correlation) between a system and its environment is involved behind the process of irreversibly progressing towards a high entropy state [ref]. Simply put, it has been pointed out that "irreversible phenomena such as a coffee cup cooling or a glass breaking are attributed to the strange quantum effect of quantum entanglement" [ref] [ref]. Specifically, if we assume that the early universe was in a very low entropy and low entanglement singular state, then as time evolves from there, entanglement increases, which can be explained as corresponding to macroscopic entropy increase and thermal equilibration [ref]. In such views, the history of relationships (entanglement) at the quantum level is at the root of irreversibility.

The process worldview provides a framework for understanding the arrow of time as network expansion and history accumulation. That is, it views the irreversible tendency of the interaction network to expand its ties of entanglement and correlation over time as being built into the universe. Informationally speaking, this is a state where "the total information (including correlation information) of the future is greater than that of the past," which is consistent with the law of entropy increase. In process ontology, the current universe is formed by the integration of processes from the past, and the arrow of time is considered to naturally appear as the history dependence of processes. For example, in Buddhist dependent origination, the twelve links starting from "ignorance" (avidyā) describe the causal chain leading to the arising of suffering (duḥkha), and a unidirectional process (flow) is also implied there. Similarly, the chain of interactions in the universe also has an initial condition dependence and a unidirectional nature. The fact that the early universe was extremely ordered (had low entropy) itself suggests that there was some singular symmetry (or lack of symmetry) in the network structure of the entire universe. In the process worldview, this is positioned as a holistic historical initial condition, and it is explained that the arrow arises as this singularity gradually diminishes with time evolution (correlations spread and homogenize).

The advantage of this explanation is that irreversibility can be understood not merely as human subjectivity (appearing so due to ignorance) but as a property of the universe's process itself. In classical theory, the arrow of time was considered merely statistical and not included in fundamental laws, but in the process worldview, the fundamental laws themselves can encompass the temporal inhomogeneity (initial condition dependence) of the network. For example, a picture such as "initially there were few interaction links and many isolated parts, but as time passed, links increased and the network became more complex." This could also be defined as "the direction in which interdependence increases" is the future direction. In the future, if research progresses to formalize this perspective and incorporate the time evolution of entanglement entropy into gravitational theory and cosmological models, it may become possible to position the arrow of time within dynamical laws.

The Black Hole Information Paradox: Resolution through Restoring Wholeness

The black hole information paradox, whether information about matter that falls into a black hole is ultimately lost or not, is a difficult problem known as a clash between quantum mechanics and general relativity. Classically, information that enters a black hole seems to be unable to escape to the outside world and is annihilated, but according to the principles of quantum mechanics, complete loss of information does not occur. There has been much discussion on how to resolve this conflict, including the holographic principle and the firewall problem.

A recent influential view is that "information is not lost but is recorded on the surface of the black hole (event horizon)" [ref]. In Susskind et al.'s holography research, information inside a black hole is said to be stored like a hologram on the event horizon, and it is expected that information will eventually appear in Hawking radiation even if the black hole evaporates [ref]. This is one logical solution to the paradox, and at least information is not lost. However, technical challenges remain, such as how information is woven into radiation (the unitary radiation spectrum after Page time) and the firewall problem associated with rapid information release.

The process worldview has the potential to resolve this paradox at an even more fundamental level. This is the direction of "relativizing the substantial boundary distinguishing the inside and outside of a black hole." The inside of a black hole is generally unobservable and thus tends to be considered an independent region, but in non-dualism, the inside and outside are merely complementary parts of the whole. If the inside of a black hole is also quantumly entangled with the outside universe, then the inside and outside are no longer completely separate systems. The recently proposed ER=EPR hypothesis (the claim that entanglement and spacetime wormholes are equivalent) is precisely an idea that attempts to avoid the information paradox by asserting that "the entangled inside and outside of a black hole are connected by a wormhole (information can effectively travel between them)" [ref]. According to this hypothesis, each pair particle of Hawking radiation is entangled with a particle inside, and since they are connected wormhole-like, information can be transmitted to the outside [ref]. In other words, radiation particles emitted from the outside of the black hole are carriers of internal information, so information is not lost.

Viewed from a process worldview, a black hole is by no means an isolated system cut off from the surrounding interaction processes but is incorporated into the universal relational network. Therefore, it is natural to think that information is dispersed in a different form within the network (phenomenally disappearing but latently remaining) rather than "disappearing." For example, even if information is extremely scrambled inside a black hole, as long as the entangled relationship with the outside is maintained, it can be considered to be latent as a correlation rather than completely disappearing. To put it extremely, the event horizon separating the inside and outside of a black hole is also merely a kind of **layer**, and from the perspective of the overall process, it may be transparent. This is an image that "a black hole is also part of the grand process of the universe, and information is conserved in the whole, including the inside." Even if information appears to be lost locally, it is highly probable that it remains in some form in the overall wave function (or the totality of relationships).

Thus, from the process worldview, the black hole information paradox can also be seen as a paradox created by an artificial substantial distinction (separation of inside and outside). If a non-dualistic connection is introduced there, the contradiction disappears, and it is reinterpreted as an interesting phenomenon where quantum and gravity merge. Indeed, since the proposal of ER=EPR, attempts to explain the relationship between the inside and outside of black holes using quantum entanglement have become active. This can be said to be a victory for the process worldview and is a good example of how philosophy, which asserts that spatially separated regions are deeply connected, is contributing to solving problems in physics.

Verification of Mathematical Consistency

When proposing new interpretations or models, it is essential to confirm that they do not contradict existing physical laws and theorems. This section discusses the consistency of the interdependent process model presented in this paper with important physical constraints (the no-signalling condition, microcausality, and conservation laws by Noether's theorem).

First, regarding the no-signalling condition. In this model, even if non-local correlations (overall relationships) are acknowledged, it does not permit the superluminal propagation of observable information. In fact, no matter how strongly quantum entanglement is interpreted, the result of a measurement performed on one side cannot be controlled and transmitted to the other [ref]. This is also true for this model; non-local correlation is merely an underlying connection for maintaining overall consistency, and on the phenomenal level, it is designed so that the causality of relativity is maintained. Therefore, the no-signalling condition (the causal structure of special relativity) is not violated in this model. Rather, under a non-dualistic worldview, the causal structure itself is positioned as a relationship within the interaction network, so the maintenance of causality itself is required as an overall state of harmony (dharma). In other words, causality is one of the rules for the process called the universe to maintain consistency, and we are merely changing the interpretation while respecting that rule set.

Next, regarding microcausality (local causality). This is a condition in quantum field theory that observables at spatially separated points commute with each other (do not influence each other). Even if non-local correlations exist, they exist only as hidden relationships that observers cannot interfere with and do not affect actual measurement values at all. Therefore, the conventional condition that the commutator of field operators is zero in spatiotemporally space-like separated regions is maintained [ref]. Even in the process model, the principle of locality, that local observables are not directly affected by anything other than local processes, is upheld. Even if everything is connected overall, the values of physical quantities appearing in subsystems change only according to the light-cone structure. In short, the model incorporates a two-layered structure where "everything is connected, but superficially appears independent and shielded (information does not leak out)," which guarantees microcausality.

Finally, consistency with conservation laws by Noether's theorem is confirmed. Noether's theorem teaches that "if the action of a system has a continuous symmetry, then there exists a corresponding conserved quantity" [ref]. Even in the process worldview, the symmetries of physical laws (time translation, spatial translation, rotation, invariants, etc.) are respected as before. Since the interpretation changes but the mathematical formulas themselves (Lagrangian or Hamiltonian) do not change, conservation laws such as energy, momentum, and angular momentum derived from symmetries are not shaken at all. Rather, in this model, the meaning of symmetries and conservation laws is reinterpreted. For example, the law of energy conservation can be re-grasped not as energy being constant in an isolated system, but from the perspective that "the amount of information (or the sum of correlations) is invariant when viewed across the entire interaction network of processes." Various conserved quantities indicated by Noether's theorem can be interpreted as the invariance of the overall structure of processes, and their validity rather supports the consistency of this model.

From the above, it has been shown that the interdependent process model does not contradict existing fundamental theorems and principles. This confirms the orientation of this model, which does not destroy physical laws with a new interpretation but rather refreshes only its philosophical interpretation while remaining within the existing system of laws.

Comparison with Conventional Models and New Prospects

Absence of Contradiction and Innovative Aspects

The proposed model does not create contradictions with current physical theories and empirical predictions. This is because the attempt at reinterpretation does not alter the physical laws themselves but replaces their philosophical interpretive framework and ontology. Therefore, for example, the results numerically derived by quantum mechanics or relativity (predictions of experimental data) are maintained as they are. The interference fringes of the double-slit experiment, the anomalous magnetic moment of the electron, and time dilation in GPS satellites are all explained in exactly the same way as by existing theories. On the other hand, the views of reality and causality assumed behind the theories are refreshed, so the way of understanding and the meaning change. This is like "retelling the same equation with a different story," and has the academic impact of changing our worldview while maintaining experimental consistency.

As a specific example, various interpretations of quantum theory such as the Copenhagen interpretation, the many-worlds interpretation, and Bohmian mechanics are all known to have completely different worldviews, although their experimental predictions themselves do not differ from quantum mechanics. This model can also be positioned as one of them. However, the characteristic of this model is that it aims for a comprehensive ontological review including not only quantum theory but also relativity and classical theory. Therefore, it can be said to aim for a paradigm shift in the entire system of physical theories, going beyond the framework of mere "interpretation." Although it currently contains no claims contrary to observed facts, if theories develop from this worldview in the future, new predictions and verifiable hypotheses may emerge. That would be a prediction based on a new paradigm and might bring about empirical differences from existing models, but at the present stage, the difference in interpretation is more prominent than contradictions between models.

The innovativeness of this model lies in changing the perspective of physics from a "catalog of entities" to a "dynamic fabric of relationships and processes." This is philosophically in line with Whitehead's process philosophy and the holistic viewpoints of 20th-century scientists. Indeed, David Bohm discussed the non-local order of quantum theory as an "implicate order" connected to Eastern thought, and John Wheeler proposed new cosmological views including information and the observing subject with slogans like "It from bit" and "Participatory Universe" [ref]. This model can be positioned as integrating these prescient insights and connecting them more systematically to modern physics. Today, due to the rise of quantum information theory and complex systems science, views not bound by traditional entity-based perspectives are emerging. This model is an interdisciplinary and philosophical attempt to re-examine the view of reality from the fundamental principles of physics within that trend and is expected to bring a breath of fresh air to scientific thought.

New Research Directions

Specific research directions opened up by the interdependent process model include the following:
	1.	Innovation in Formal Approaches: Conventional theories have been formulated based on differential equations and action principles, but the process model has room to introduce network mathematics and category theory methods. For example, related frameworks have already been proposed, such as "reconstruction of quantum mechanics by monoidal categories" that express quantum processes with category theory, and the "process matrix" formalism (Oreshkov et al.'s approach) that handles the indefiniteness of temporal order. It is desirable to advance these and construct a new mathematical system that captures physical phenomena as mappings of networks.
	2.	Fusion with Information Theory: Though not Wheeler's "It from bit," by thoroughly adopting the perspective of regarding physical existence as information processes, research connecting the development of quantum computation and quantum communication with fundamental theory can progress. Even in the context of quantum gravity, informational perspectives such as the entropy of spacetime and spacetime construction by quantum entanglement are hot topics [ref]. The process worldview has the potential to lead to an ultimate understanding of physical laws as self-organizing phenomena of information and is also related to Wolfram's recent attempts (theory of everything by hypergraphs). In the future, it is expected that the fusion area of physics and information science will deepen further, and research to embody the scheme "matter = information = process" will be anticipated.
	3.	Integration with Other Fields: Process philosophy also influences biology and cognitive science, and there is a movement to understand living systems as processes (process biology). If physics becomes process-based, a common language with complex systems including life and consciousness may emerge. For example, a path may open to understand the hard problem of consciousness as a new phase of physical processes (information integration or patterns of relationships). Although this paper did not directly discuss consciousness, philosophically, since non-dualism advocates the fusion of subject and object, it may contribute to a unified understanding of mind and matter in the long term.
	4.	Contribution to Education and Conceptual Understanding: This worldview might also be useful in physics education. If the counter-intuitiveness of quantum theory and the spacetime structure of relativity are explained with non-dualistic analogies (e.g., "network of relationships," "all things are connected"), students with philosophical background knowledge may find them easier to grasp. In fact, when teaching dynamical systems, quoting Wheeler's words [ref] to convey that "gravity is a phenomenon resulting from interaction" makes it easier to imagine the interdependence of space and matter. By bridging advanced concepts in physics and the intuitive insights of Eastern philosophy, it may contribute to promoting understanding in education and dialogue between science and humanities.

Finally, it should be noted that this model isあくまで (akumade - to the last, consistently) an interpretative framework. It does not directly provide experimental predictions itself. However, looking back at history, there are many examples where a shift in worldview, such as Copernicus's heliocentric theory or Einstein's view of spacetime, has led to scientific progress. Therefore, a refresh of interpretation can be an important step towards new theories. It is hoped that the non-dualistic process worldview proposed in this paper will also function as a matrix that expands the freedom of thought in the future development of physics.

Conclusion

This paper attempted to reinterpret the theoretical structure of modern physics as a "shared rule set consisting of interdependent processes," based on the non-dualistic perspectives of Upanishadic philosophy and Buddhism. Each element of quantum mechanics, relativity, and classical mechanics was repositioned not as a static entity but as a process on a network of relationships, and it was shown that quantum phenomena such as wave function collapse and entanglement can also be understood as process updates. Regarding the non-locality indicated by Bell's theorem, it was discussed that by abandoning adherence to realism and locality, it can be naturally accepted under non-dualism. Furthermore, from this worldview, new light was shed on difficult problems such as quantum gravity, the measurement problem, dark components, the arrow of time, and the black hole information problem, and the possibility was described that paradoxes arising from the conventional entity-based framework would be alleviated. The proposed model is mathematically consistent with the current theoretical system (it does not violate the no-signalling principle or conservation laws) and is innovative mainly at the level of interpretation.

This research is an ambitious attempt to integrate scientific knowledge with Eastern philosophical intuition by re-examining the ontology implicitly adopted by physics. Of course, the presented worldview itself is currently only a hypothetical framework, and further philosophical refinement and mathematical concretization will be necessary in the future. However, the significance of this research is great in that it can provide a new perspective on the complex problems facing 21st-century physics. The non-dualistic insight that **"all things are in a process of mutual generation"** has been shown to be not merely a metaphor but a description of reality in deciphering the mysteries of quantum theory and the depths of cosmology [ref] [ref]. At the same time, it will also serve as a bridge connecting the philosophy cultivated by human wisdom and cutting-edge science.

Erwin Schrödinger, in his later years, leaned towards Vedanta (Hindu philosophy) and is said to have stated, "The mind is only one." He was a founder of quantum mechanics and was also deeply influenced by Eastern thought. If we follow the insights of such predecessors, the ultimate goal of physics, a unified theory, may mean not only the unification of equations but also the unification of worldviews [ref] [ref]. It is hoped that the process worldview proposed in this paper will become a stepping stone towards that unification and contribute to spinning a new story of science.
